Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Avalanche vs Snowball method?
Avalanche: Pay off the debt with the highest interest rate first. This saves you the most money over time. Snowball: Pay off the smallest balance first. This gives you quick wins that build motivation. Both methods require paying all minimum payments first, then throwing extra money at your priority debt. Mathematically, Avalanche almost always wins — but Snowball works better psychologically for many people.
What are the typical credit card interest rates in the Philippines?
Most Philippine banks charge 1.85%-2.5% per month (approximately 22%-30% annually) on unpaid credit card balances. The BSP cap is 3% per month (36% annually). Digital lenders like GCash GCredit (5%/month) and BillEase (3.49%/month) tend to charge significantly higher rates. Always check your latest statement for your actual rate.
How much of my salary should go to debt payments?
Financial advisors recommend keeping total debt payments (including rent/mortgage) below 36% of your gross income. For unsecured debt like credit cards and personal loans, aim for under 20%. If your debt payments exceed 50% of take-home pay, consider debt consolidation or speaking with a financial counselor.
Should I save or pay debt first?
Build a small emergency fund first (₱5,000-₱10,000) to avoid taking on new debt when emergencies happen. Then aggressively pay down high-interest debt (anything above 2%/month). Once high-interest debt is cleared, split your extra money between building a full 3-month emergency fund and paying off remaining low-interest debt.
